## Abstract

As artificial intelligence (AI) tools rapidly proliferate across K-12 educational settings, a critical gap persists in understanding how parents---particularly those from historically underserved communities---perceive, understand, and feel about AI's role in their children's education. This descriptive, cross-sectional study examined the perceptions of 113 parents from predominantly Latino and English Language Learner (ELL) communities in two Southern California school districts: Culver City Unified School District and Lynwood Unified School District. ## Introduction

The integration of artificial intelligence into K-12 education represents one of the most significant shifts in instructional technology since the introduction of personal computing in classrooms. From adaptive learning platforms such as Khan Academy's Khanmigo to generative AI tools like ChatGPT, AI-powered technologies are reshaping how students learn, how teachers instruct, and how educational institutions operate (Holmes et al., 2023; Luckin et al., 2016). The U.S. Department of Education's Office of Educational Technology (2023) has acknowledged both the transformative potential and the risks of AI in education, calling for thoughtful, equity-centered approaches to integration. Yet amid the rapid deployment of these technologies, one critical stakeholder group remains largely unheard: parents.

### The Parent Perspective Gap

Parents serve as essential gatekeepers, advocates, and co-educators in their children's learning experiences (Epstein, 2011). Their perceptions of educational technologies significantly influence adoption patterns, home learning environments, and the degree to which school-based technology initiatives succeed or falter (Hollingworth et al., 2011; Sánchez et al., 2018). Despite this well-documented influence, the research base on parent perceptions of AI in education remains remarkably thin. A growing body of work examines teacher attitudes toward AI (Celik et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2021), student experiences with AI-powered tools (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019), and administrator decision-making around AI adoption (Bebell & O'Dwyer, 2010). Comparatively few studies have systematically asked parents what they know, think, and feel about AI entering their children's classrooms.

This gap is particularly consequential given that parent buy-in---or resistance---can make or break technology integration efforts. When parents do not understand a technology, feel excluded from decisions about its use, or harbor concerns that go unaddressed, the resulting friction can undermine even the most well-designed school initiatives (Povey et al., 2016). Conversely, when parents are informed, engaged, and supportive, technology integration proceeds more smoothly and yields greater benefits for students (Ribble, 2015).

### The Latino/Hispanic Parent Gap

The absence of parent voices in AI education research is most acute for Latino and Hispanic families. Latino students now constitute approximately 28% of all public school enrollment in the United States, making them the largest minority group in the K-12 system (National Center for Education Statistics [NCES], 2023). In California, where this study was conducted, Latino students represent over 55% of the K-12 population (California Department of Education, 2024). Yet research on how Latino parents perceive emerging educational technologies---and AI specifically---is virtually nonexistent.

This gap matters for several intersecting reasons. First, Latino families are disproportionately affected by the digital divide, with lower rates of broadband access, fewer devices per household, and less exposure to advanced digital tools compared to White and Asian American families (Perrin & Turner, 2019; Warschauer & Tate, 2018). Second, language barriers create additional obstacles: many Latino parents are Spanish-dominant or have limited English proficiency, meaning that school communications about AI---which are overwhelmingly produced in English and laden with technical jargon---may never reach them in an accessible form (Gonzalez et al., 2013). Third, historical patterns of institutional distrust, rooted in experiences of discrimination, deficit-based narratives, and exclusion from decision-making, can shape how Latino parents engage with unfamiliar school initiatives (Valencia, 2010; Yosso, 2005).

At the same time, research consistently demonstrates that Latino parents hold high educational aspirations for their children and are deeply invested in their academic success (Ceballo et al., 2014; Goldenberg et al., 2001). The challenge is not a lack of parental interest but rather a lack of institutional effort to include these families in meaningful, culturally responsive ways (Delgado-Gaitan, 2004). Understanding Latino parent perceptions of AI is therefore not merely an academic exercise; it is an equity imperative.

### Cultural Considerations

Any investigation of Latino parent perspectives on AI in education must account for the cultural, linguistic, and socioeconomic contexts that shape those perspectives. Several considerations are particularly salient.

**Language and communication.** For parents whose primary language is Spanish, the technical vocabulary surrounding AI---terms like "machine learning," "algorithm," "generative AI," and "large language model"---may be unfamiliar even in English, let alone in translation. Effective communication about AI requires not only linguistic translation but also conceptual translation: explaining what AI does in concrete, relatable terms connected to parents' lived experiences (Moll et al., 1992).

**Trust and institutional relationships.** Latino parents' relationships with schools are often mediated by historical and ongoing experiences with institutional power. Research on Latino parent engagement consistently finds that many parents feel unwelcome, dismissed, or positioned as passive recipients of information rather than active partners (Auerbach, 2007; Olivos, 2006). Introducing AI---a technology that many adults find opaque and unsettling---into this relational context demands particular attention to trust-building.

**Digital literacy and access.** While smartphone ownership among Latino adults has reached near parity with other demographic groups, disparities persist in broadband access, device quality, and the types of digital skills that parents possess (Vogels, 2021). Parents who primarily use technology for communication and entertainment may have limited frameworks for evaluating AI as an educational tool.

**Funds of knowledge.** Moll et al. (1992) and González et al. (2005) introduced the concept of "funds of knowledge" to describe the rich cultural, cognitive, and practical resources that families from non-dominant communities possess. Latino parents bring valuable perspectives---about education, about their children, about technology, about risk---that are assets, not deficits. Research that positions these parents as knowledgeable agents rather than uninformed subjects is both methodologically stronger and ethically necessary.

### Purpose and Research Questions

The purpose of this descriptive study was to examine how parents from predominantly Latino communities in Southern California perceive artificial intelligence in the context of their children's K-12 education. Specifically, the study sought to answer the following research questions:

1. **RQ1:** What is the current level of AI familiarity among Latino parents in the participating school communities?
2. **RQ2:** What attitudes do these parents hold toward the use of AI in their children's education, and how do these attitudes vary by grade level of the child?
3. **RQ3:** What are the primary concerns Latino parents express about AI in education, and how are these concerns distributed across the sample?
4. **RQ4:** What do Latino parents want to learn about AI, and what learning outcomes do they desire for their children in relation to AI?

By addressing these questions, this study aims to provide an empirical foundation for school districts, policymakers, and researchers seeking to engage Latino families in the ongoing conversation about AI in education.

## Literature Review

### AI in K-12 Education: The Current Landscape

Artificial intelligence in education (AIEd) has evolved from a niche research area to a mainstream concern of educational practice and policy. The current wave of AI adoption in K-12 schools encompasses several distinct categories of tools: intelligent tutoring systems that adapt to individual learner needs (VanLehn, 2011), automated writing evaluation tools (Wilson & Czik, 2016), AI-powered learning management systems, chatbots for student support, and---most visibly since late 2022---generative AI tools capable of producing text, images, code, and other content in response to natural language prompts (Kasneci et al., 2023).

The release of ChatGPT in November 2022 catalyzed a rapid acceleration in AI adoption across educational contexts. Within months, surveys indicated that significant percentages of students were using generative AI for schoolwork (Walton Family Foundation, 2023), and school districts were scrambling to develop policies governing its use. Some districts initially banned generative AI tools; others embraced them cautiously; most found themselves navigating uncharted territory without clear guidance or empirical evidence to inform their decisions (Trust et al., 2023).

The scholarly response has been substantial but uneven. A growing literature examines the pedagogical affordances and risks of AI in education (Holmes et al., 2023; Selwyn, 2019), the ethical dimensions of algorithmic decision-making in schools (Williamson, 2017), and the potential for AI to either exacerbate or ameliorate educational inequities (Reich, 2020). Teacher perspectives have received considerable attention, with studies documenting a range of attitudes from enthusiastic adoption to deep skepticism (Celik et al., 2022; Scherer et al., 2019). Student experiences with AI tools have also been explored, particularly in higher education contexts (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019).

Notably absent from this growing evidence base, however, are the perspectives of parents---the stakeholders who ultimately decide whether and how AI tools are used in home learning environments, who shape children's attitudes toward technology, and whose consent and support are prerequisites for school-based AI initiatives.

### Parent Engagement in Educational Technology Adoption

The broader literature on parent engagement in educational technology provides a useful framework for understanding the current AI moment. Research consistently demonstrates that parents play a mediating role in children's technology use, shaping both the quantity and quality of engagement through their own attitudes, knowledge, and practices (Livingstone & Helsper, 2008; Nikken & Jansz, 2014). Parental mediation theory identifies several strategies parents employ: restrictive mediation (setting rules and limits), active mediation (discussing content and co-using), and technical mediation (using filtering or monitoring tools) (Livingstone & Helsper, 2008).

Studies of parent attitudes toward educational technology more broadly have found that most parents are generally supportive of technology in schools but harbor specific concerns about screen time, online safety, and the quality of digital content (Rideout & Robb, 2019). Importantly, parent attitudes are not monolithic; they vary by socioeconomic status, education level, cultural background, and personal experience with technology (Hargittai, 2002; Warschauer, 2004).

The COVID-19 pandemic dramatically altered the parent-technology relationship. Forced into the role of de facto technology coordinators during remote learning, many parents developed new perspectives---both positive and negative---on the role of digital tools in education (Garbe et al., 2020). For families in under-resourced communities, the pandemic exposed and deepened existing digital divides, with many parents struggling to support remote learning due to connectivity issues, device shortages, and unfamiliarity with learning platforms (Dorn et al., 2020). The pandemic experience is critical context for understanding current parent attitudes toward AI: many parents are simultaneously more aware of technology's role in education and more attuned to its limitations and risks.

### Latino Parent Engagement in Education

A robust body of scholarship examines Latino parent engagement in education, challenging deficit narratives that frame Latino parents as uninvolved or disinterested. Epstein's (2011) widely cited framework identifies six types of parent involvement: parenting, communicating, volunteering, learning at home, decision-making, and collaborating with the community. Research consistently finds that Latino parents engage in all six types, though their engagement may look different from dominant-culture norms and may go unrecognized by schools (Auerbach, 2007; Delgado-Gaitan, 2004).

Valencia (2010) and Yosso (2005) have been particularly influential in reframing discussions of Latino parent engagement. Valencia's critique of deficit thinking argues that schools often misinterpret linguistic and cultural differences as evidence of parental deficiency, obscuring the rich educational practices that occur within Latino homes. Yosso's concept of "community cultural wealth" identifies multiple forms of capital---aspirational, linguistic, familial, social, navigational, and resistant---that Latino families draw upon to support their children's education.

Several studies have examined Latino parent engagement with technology specifically. Katz and Gonzalez (2016) found that low-income Latino parents actively supported their children's digital literacy despite limited access and English proficiency, employing creative strategies such as relying on bilingual children as "brokers" for technology-related tasks. Sánchez et al. (2018) documented how Spanish-speaking parents navigated school communication platforms, highlighting both the barriers they faced and the resourcefulness they demonstrated. Warschauer and Tate (2018) examined the intersection of language, culture, and digital access in Latino communities, finding that purely access-focused interventions were insufficient without attention to cultural and linguistic factors.

### Digital Equity and the Cultural Digital Divide

The concept of the digital divide has evolved considerably since its initial framing as a binary between technology "haves" and "have-nots." Contemporary scholarship distinguishes among first-level digital divides (access to hardware and connectivity), second-level divides (differences in digital skills and usage patterns), and third-level divides (differential outcomes from technology use) (Hargittai, 2002; Wei et al., 2011). For Latino communities, all three levels of the digital divide remain relevant.

Warschauer (2004) argued that the digital divide is best understood not as a technological problem but as a social one, rooted in the same structural inequities that produce disparities in income, education, and health. This framing is particularly apt for AI: as AI tools become increasingly embedded in educational settings, the question is not merely whether Latino families have access to AI-powered tools but whether they have the knowledge, language, and institutional support to evaluate, navigate, and benefit from them.

The concept of "algorithmic literacy"---the ability to understand how algorithms work, what data they use, and how they influence one's experiences---has emerged as a critical component of digital literacy in the AI age (Dogruel et al., 2022). For parents making decisions about their children's engagement with AI-powered educational tools, algorithmic literacy is not an abstract concern; it directly affects their ability to provide informed consent, set appropriate boundaries, and advocate for their children's interests.

### The Gap: Latino Parent Perceptions of AI

Despite the extensive literatures on AI in education, parent engagement in technology adoption, and Latino parent involvement, virtually no published research directly examines how Latino parents perceive AI in the context of their children's education. This gap exists at the intersection of three rapidly evolving areas of scholarship and practice, and its consequences are significant.

Without empirical data on Latino parent perspectives, school districts implementing AI tools are operating on assumptions about what parents know, want, and fear. Policymakers developing AI governance frameworks for education lack input from the communities most likely to be affected by both the benefits and the risks of these technologies. Researchers studying AI in education are building theoretical models that exclude the perspectives of the nation's largest minority parent population.

This study represents an initial step toward filling this gap. By documenting the AI familiarity, attitudes, concerns, and learning desires of 113 parents from predominantly Latino communities in Southern California, it provides a descriptive foundation for future research, policy, and practice.

## Methodology

### Research Design

This study employed a descriptive, cross-sectional survey design to capture a snapshot of parent perceptions of AI in K-12 education at a specific point in time. Descriptive research is appropriate when the goal is to document the characteristics, attitudes, or behaviors of a population, particularly when the phenomenon under study is new or under-researched (Creswell & Creswell, 2018). Given the near-total absence of prior data on Latino parent perceptions of AI, a descriptive approach was selected as a necessary precursor to more complex inferential or experimental designs.

### Participants

The sample consisted of 113 parents from two school districts in Southern California: Culver City Unified School District (CCUSD) and Lynwood Unified School District (LUSD). Both districts serve predominantly Latino student populations and include significant proportions of English Language Learners.

Culver City USD, located in western Los Angeles County, serves approximately 7,000 students. While historically more diverse in its demographic composition, the district includes schools with substantial Latino enrollment and has been actively pursuing equity-focused initiatives. Lynwood USD, located in southeastern Los Angeles County, serves approximately 13,000 students, with Latino students comprising over 90% of enrollment and a large proportion designated as English Language Learners.

Data were collected across two survey administrations: a primary administration yielding 103 responses and a secondary administration in January 2026 yielding 10 responses, for a combined total of 113 participants.

### Instrument

The survey instrument was developed collaboratively by the research team in consultation with school site administrators and parent liaisons. The instrument was hosted on the Smartsheet platform and included both structured (closed-ended) and open-ended items. The survey was designed with accessibility as a priority, using clear, jargon-free language and providing a Spanish-language version for parents who preferred it.

The survey measured the following constructs:

1. **AI Familiarity.** A single item assessed parents' self-reported familiarity with artificial intelligence on a scale from "Not familiar at all" to "Very familiar."

2. **Attitude Toward AI in Education.** Parents selected the descriptor that best characterized their overall attitude: Supportive, Curious, Cautious, or Concerned.

3. **Comfort Level.** Parents rated their comfort with their child using AI tools in school.

4. **Concerns About AI.** Parents selected from a list of potential concerns, including: privacy and data safety, plagiarism and overreliance, accuracy of AI-generated content, loss of critical thinking skills, screen time, and other (with write-in option). Multiple selections were permitted.

5. **Desired Learning Outcomes.** Parents indicated what they hoped their children would gain from AI-integrated education.

6. **Personal AI Use.** A binary item asked whether parents had personally used AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Siri, Google Assistant).

7. **What Parents Want to Learn.** An open-ended item asked parents to describe what they would like to learn about AI in the context of their children's education.

8. **Grade Level.** Parents indicated the grade band of their child (TK-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-12).

### Data Collection Procedures

Data were collected during parent-facing AI professional development sessions organized by The Right Path Educational Consulting (TRPEC) at participating school sites. These sessions were designed to introduce parents to AI concepts, demonstrate common AI tools, and facilitate discussion about AI's role in education. The survey was administered at the beginning of each session, prior to any instructional content, to capture baseline perceptions uninfluenced by the session.

The sessions were conducted in bilingual (English/Spanish) format, and bilingual facilitators were available to assist parents with survey completion. Parents were informed that their responses would be used for research purposes and that participation was voluntary. No identifying information (names, student IDs) was collected, ensuring respondent anonymity.

### Data Analysis

Data analysis proceeded in two phases. Quantitative analysis employed descriptive statistics, including frequencies, percentages, means, and standard deviations, to characterize the distribution of responses across survey items. Cross-tabulations were conducted to examine potential patterns by grade level of the child. All quantitative analyses were conducted using standard statistical software.

Qualitative analysis of open-ended responses employed thematic analysis following the approach outlined by Braun and Clarke (2006). Responses were read iteratively, initial codes were generated, codes were organized into candidate themes, and themes were reviewed and refined. For responses provided in Spanish, translation was conducted by a bilingual member of the research team prior to coding, with attention to preserving the original meaning and nuance of the responses.

### Ethical Considerations

This study was conducted in accordance with ethical principles for research with human subjects. Survey participation was voluntary and anonymous. No data were collected from minors. The research team obtained appropriate approvals from participating school sites prior to data collection.

### Limitations of the Methodology

Several methodological limitations should be noted at the outset. First, the sample was a convenience sample of parents who chose to attend AI professional development sessions, which may introduce selection bias: parents who attend such sessions may be more interested in, knowledgeable about, or open to educational technology than the broader parent population. Second, the study captures only pre-session perceptions; without post-session data or matched identifiers, it is not possible to assess changes in perception resulting from the professional development experience. Third, the sample, while meaningful for a descriptive study, is relatively small (N = 113) and drawn from only two school districts, limiting generalizability. Fourth, the two survey administrations (primary and January 2026) occurred at different time points, and the rapidly evolving public discourse around AI means that perceptions may shift over relatively short periods. Fifth, the survey instrument, while developed with input from school personnel and parent liaisons, did not undergo formal psychometric validation (e.g., reliability analysis, factor analysis).

These limitations notwithstanding, the study provides valuable initial data on a topic for which virtually no prior empirical evidence exists.

## Discussion

The findings of this study provide an initial empirical window into how parents from predominantly Latino communities perceive artificial intelligence in the context of their children's K-12 education. While descriptive in nature, the results illuminate patterns that have significant implications for research, policy, and practice. This discussion situates the findings within the existing literature, addresses cultural implications specific to Latino families, and considers how the results inform district-level AI integration efforts.

### Interpreting Parent Familiarity and Attitudes

[INTERPRETATION TO BE REFINED AFTER DATA INSERTION]

The distribution of AI familiarity reported by parents in this study likely reflects broader patterns documented in the digital equity literature. Warschauer (2004) argued that technology familiarity is not merely a function of access but is shaped by the social, cultural, and economic contexts in which individuals encounter technology. For many Latino parents in the participating communities, exposure to AI may be largely incidental---encountering voice assistants, recommendation algorithms, or autocorrect features without framing these experiences as "AI." This finding, if confirmed by the data, underscores the importance of meeting parents where they are: beginning AI education efforts with concrete, relatable examples rather than abstract technical concepts.

The attitude distribution---across the Supportive, Curious, Cautious, and Concerned categories---is anticipated to reveal a population that is neither uniformly enthusiastic nor uniformly resistant. This nuanced attitudinal landscape aligns with the broader technology adoption literature, which consistently finds that attitudes toward educational technology are multidimensional and context-dependent (Ertmer & Ottenbreit-Leftwich, 2010). The likely presence of a substantial "Curious" cohort is particularly noteworthy, as it suggests a population that is open to learning more---a finding with direct implications for the design of parent engagement initiatives.

### Cultural Implications for Latino Families

The findings of this study must be understood within the cultural context of the participating communities. Several considerations are particularly relevant.

**The role of familismo.** In many Latino families, educational decisions are made collectively, with input from extended family members and strong emphasis on the well-being of the family unit (Calzada et al., 2010). Concerns about AI---particularly regarding privacy, safety, and the potential for technology to disrupt family relationships---may be amplified by familismo values that prioritize collective well-being over individual advancement. School districts introducing AI tools should consider how to address not only individual parent concerns but also the broader family system within which those concerns are situated.

**Confianza (trust) and institutional relationships.** The degree to which parents express concern versus support for AI in education may be mediated by their level of confianza---trust---in the schools and districts serving their children. Latino parents who have experienced welcoming, respectful, and responsive school environments may be more open to AI integration, while those who have encountered deficit-based attitudes or exclusionary practices may approach AI with greater skepticism (Auerbach, 2007). The professional development sessions at which these surveys were administered represent one model for building confianza: by creating spaces where parents can learn, ask questions, and express concerns in their home language, schools signal that parent perspectives matter.

**Funds of knowledge and AI literacy.** Moll et al. (1992) demonstrated that Latino households possess rich funds of knowledge---practical skills, cultural practices, and ways of knowing---that are often invisible to schools. As AI literacy becomes an increasingly important form of cultural capital, it is essential that educational institutions recognize and build upon the knowledge and experiences that Latino parents already possess. A parent who has navigated complex bureaucratic systems, translated between languages and cultural contexts, or evaluated the reliability of information from multiple sources has developed cognitive skills directly relevant to AI literacy, even if they have never used an AI tool by name.

### Implications for AI Integration Policies

The concerns expressed by parents in this study carry direct implications for how school districts develop and communicate their AI integration policies. If privacy and data safety emerge as primary concerns---as is anticipated based on prior technology adoption research---districts will need to provide clear, accessible, multilingual information about how student data is collected, stored, and used by AI tools. Generic assurances of compliance with FERPA and COPPA are insufficient; parents need to understand, in concrete terms, what data their children's AI tools access and what protections are in place.

Similarly, concerns about plagiarism and overreliance suggest that parents are thinking critically about the pedagogical implications of AI---not merely its novelty. This finding, if confirmed, challenges deficit narratives that assume parents from under-resourced communities are uncritical consumers of technology. It also suggests that parent education efforts should go beyond basic AI awareness to engage parents as partners in developing norms for appropriate AI use in academic work.

### Connection to Technology-Enhanced Instruction for Multilingual Learners

The findings of this study resonate with the broader framework of technology-enhanced instruction for multilingual learners, an area in which the lead author has conducted prior research (Martin, [YEAR]). [VERIFY: reference to Martin's dissertation]. That framework emphasizes the importance of designing technology interventions that account for the linguistic, cultural, and socioeconomic realities of diverse learner populations---a principle equally applicable to parent education about AI.

Multilingual learners and their families occupy a unique position in the AI landscape. On one hand, AI tools offer significant potential for supporting language development, providing personalized vocabulary instruction, enabling translation and communication, and adapting content to varying levels of language proficiency. On the other hand, these same families may face heightened risks from AI, including bias in language models that privilege Standard American English, privacy concerns related to voice data collection, and the perpetuation of stereotypes in AI-generated content (Bender et al., 2021).

The dual nature of AI's potential for multilingual communities---simultaneously promising and perilous---underscores the importance of centering parent voices in decisions about AI adoption. Parents who understand both the benefits and the risks of AI are better positioned to advocate for their children and to hold schools accountable for equitable implementation.

### The Role of Parent Professional Development

The context in which this data was collected---parent-facing AI professional development sessions---is itself a finding worth discussing. The existence of these sessions reflects a growing recognition among school leaders that parent education is a necessary component of AI integration. Yet the model is far from universal: most school districts have not yet developed structured programs to educate parents about AI, and those that have often struggle to reach the families who would benefit most (Livingstone et al., 2017).

The survey data, combined with the qualitative responses about what parents want to learn, can inform the design of more effective parent PD programs. Key design principles suggested by the data include:

- **Bilingual delivery.** Sessions and materials must be available in the languages spoken by the parent community.
- **Hands-on experience.** Parents benefit from actually using AI tools in a supported environment, rather than merely hearing about them.
- **Concrete examples.** Abstract discussions of "algorithms" or "machine learning" are less effective than demonstrations of how AI tools are used in their child's specific grade level and subject area.
- **Space for concerns.** Effective PD creates structured opportunities for parents to voice concerns and receive honest, non-dismissive responses.
- **Ongoing engagement.** A single session is insufficient; sustained engagement over time is needed to build knowledge, comfort, and trust.

---

## Implications for Practice

The findings of this study, while preliminary, point to several actionable recommendations for school districts, administrators, and educators serving Latino and ELL communities.

### 1. Develop Bilingual AI Communication Strategies

School districts should ensure that all communications about AI---from policy announcements to classroom newsletters to consent forms---are available in both English and Spanish (and other home languages as needed). These communications should avoid technical jargon and explain AI concepts using relatable, everyday examples. Bilingual parent liaisons and community health workers can serve as cultural brokers who translate not only language but also meaning.

### 2. Create Structured Parent AI Education Programs

Districts should invest in ongoing parent education programs focused on AI, modeled on the professional development sessions from which this study's data were drawn. Effective programs should include:

- **Multiple sessions** rather than one-time events
- **Hands-on AI tool demonstrations** relevant to children's grade levels
- **Family learning opportunities** where parents and children explore AI together
- **Community-based venues** (churches, community centers, libraries) in addition to school sites
- **Childcare and food** to reduce barriers to attendance

### 3. Center Parent Voices in AI Policy Development

Districts developing AI use policies should include Latino parents in the process, not merely as recipients of finished policies but as co-creators. Parent advisory committees, community forums, and surveys conducted in home languages can ensure that policy reflects the values and concerns of the families most affected. The data from this study can serve as a starting point for these conversations.

### 4. Address the Trust Gap

For schools serving communities with histories of institutional distrust, AI integration efforts must be grounded in relationship-building. This means:

- Acknowledging parents' concerns as legitimate and rational
- Providing transparent information about data practices
- Demonstrating accountability when problems arise
- Engaging trusted community leaders and organizations as partners
- Avoiding deficit-based framing that positions parents as obstacles to progress

### 5. Leverage Funds of Knowledge

Schools should recognize and build upon the knowledge and experiences that Latino parents bring to conversations about AI. A parent who has evaluated the reliability of health information online, navigated a complex immigration system, or managed a household budget with limited resources has developed critical thinking and evaluation skills directly transferable to AI literacy. Effective parent education programs frame these experiences as assets rather than deficits.

### 6. Differentiate by Grade Level

If the cross-tabulation data reveal meaningful differences in parent attitudes or concerns by grade level---as is hypothesized---districts should tailor their communication and engagement strategies accordingly. Parents of younger children (TK-2) may need different information than parents of high schoolers, both in terms of content (what AI tools their children will encounter) and framing (developmental appropriateness, screen time considerations vs. college and career readiness).

## Limitations and Future Research

### Limitations

This study has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the findings.

**Convenience sampling.** Participants were parents who chose to attend AI professional development sessions at their children's schools. This self-selected sample may overrepresent parents who are more engaged with school activities, more interested in technology, or more comfortable in school settings. Parents who do not attend school events---who may face greater barriers related to work schedules, transportation, language, or institutional distrust---are underrepresented. Their perspectives, which may differ significantly from those captured here, remain unknown.

**Pre-survey only.** The data represent pre-session perceptions only. Without post-session data, it is not possible to assess whether the professional development sessions changed parent attitudes, increased familiarity, or addressed concerns. The absence of matched pre-post pairs (due to survey anonymity) further limits the ability to track individual-level change.

**Sample size and generalizability.** With 113 respondents from two school districts in Southern California, the sample is sufficient for descriptive purposes but too small for robust inferential statistics or generalization to the broader population of Latino parents in the United States. Regional factors---including California's particular demographic composition, technology infrastructure, and educational policy environment---may limit applicability to other contexts.

**Cross-sectional design.** As a single-time-point snapshot, the study cannot capture how parent perceptions of AI evolve over time. Given the rapid pace of AI development and the shifting public discourse around AI, perceptions measured in 2025-2026 may differ substantially from those held even six months later.

**Instrument validation.** The survey instrument was developed for this specific context and has not undergone formal psychometric validation. While the items were reviewed by the research team and school-based practitioners for face validity and clarity, no reliability analyses (e.g., Cronbach's alpha) or factor analyses were conducted.

**Demographic granularity.** The survey did not collect detailed demographic data such as parent education level, income, immigration status, or primary language. These variables are known to influence technology attitudes and would allow for more nuanced analyses of subgroup differences.

### Directions for Future Research

This study opens several avenues for future investigation.

**Pre-post intervention studies.** A natural extension of this work would be to administer matched pre- and post-surveys surrounding parent AI professional development sessions, using unique identifiers to track individual-level change. Such studies could assess the efficacy of different PD models and identify which components of parent education are most effective at increasing AI literacy and comfort.

**Expanded geographic and demographic scope.** Replicating this study across a broader range of school districts---including rural, suburban, and urban contexts; districts in different states; and districts with varying demographic compositions---would strengthen the evidence base and support generalization.

**Qualitative depth.** While the open-ended survey items in this study provide some qualitative data, in-depth interviews and focus groups with Latino parents would yield richer understanding of the beliefs, values, and experiences that shape their perceptions of AI. Ethnographic approaches that observe parent-school interactions around AI would be particularly valuable.

**Longitudinal designs.** Tracking parent perceptions over multiple time points---ideally over the course of a full academic year or longer---would capture how perceptions evolve as parents gain more experience with AI tools, receive more information from schools, and observe their children's engagement with AI.

**Student perspectives.** This study focused exclusively on parents. Companion studies examining the perspectives of their children---the students who directly interact with AI tools in school---would provide a more complete picture of how Latino families navigate AI in education.

**Cross-cultural comparisons.** Comparative studies examining AI perceptions across different racial/ethnic groups, socioeconomic strata, and linguistic communities would illuminate both the universal and culturally specific dimensions of parent attitudes toward AI in education.

**Instrument development.** The field would benefit from a rigorously validated survey instrument for measuring parent AI perceptions, developed through standard psychometric procedures (item analysis, reliability testing, factor analysis) and validated across diverse populations. Such an instrument would enable cross-study comparisons and meta-analyses.

---

## Conclusion

Artificial intelligence is no longer a speculative future for K-12 education; it is a present reality, reshaping classrooms, curricula, and the expectations placed on students and families. Yet the voices of the parents who must navigate this reality---particularly parents from communities that have historically been excluded from conversations about educational technology---remain largely unheard in the research literature.

This study represents an initial effort to center the perspectives of Latino parents in the discourse about AI in education. By documenting the familiarity, attitudes, concerns, and learning desires of 113 parents from predominantly Latino communities in Southern California, it provides a descriptive foundation upon which future research can build. The findings underscore the complexity of parent perceptions: these are not uniformly enthusiastic or uniformly resistant parents but thoughtful individuals navigating an unfamiliar technological landscape with the tools, knowledge, and cultural resources available to them.

The study also highlights the critical importance of parent education and engagement in AI integration efforts. Schools and districts that invest in bilingual, culturally responsive, ongoing parent education about AI are more likely to achieve genuine partnership with the families they serve. Those that do not risk deepening the very divides that equitable AI integration should seek to close.

As AI continues to evolve and its presence in educational settings expands, the need for research that includes all stakeholders---including parents, including Latino families, including communities that have too often been studied about but not with---will only grow. This study is one step in that direction.
