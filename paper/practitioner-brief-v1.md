# What 113 Parents Told Us About AI in Their Children's Schools

## A Program Evaluation Brief from Parent AI Professional Development Sessions in Southern California

**Charles Martin, Ed.D.**
Alexandria's Design | Carlsbad, California

**William Gideon Jr., Ed.D.**
The Right Path Educational Consulting (TRPEC) | Southern California

---

## Overview

Between May 2025 and January 2026, The Right Path Educational Consulting (TRPEC) facilitated a series of parent-facing professional development sessions on artificial intelligence in K-12 education at schools in Culver City Unified School District and Lynwood Unified School District, California. Before each session, parents completed a structured survey measuring their familiarity with AI, attitudes toward AI in their children's education, comfort levels, concerns, and desired learning outcomes.

This brief presents findings from 113 parent responses collected across two survey administrations. It is intended to help school districts, parent engagement coordinators, and educational technology leaders understand what parents are thinking and feeling about AI -- and how to design better engagement around it.

**Note on community context:** The participating districts serve diverse communities. Lynwood USD enrolls a student population that is approximately 97% Hispanic/Latino, and Culver City USD serves a mixed-demographic community. Many participating families include English Language Learners. These demographic characteristics are relevant context for interpreting the findings, though individual demographic data was not collected in the survey instrument.

---

## Why This Matters

AI tools are entering classrooms faster than schools can develop policies around them. Teachers are being trained. Students are experimenting. But in most districts, parents are the last to know. They hear about AI from the news, from their kids, or not at all.

This is a problem because:

- **Parents control the home learning environment.** If they don't understand AI, they can't guide their children's use of it.
- **Parent buy-in determines policy success.** AI integration efforts that ignore parents risk backlash, opt-outs, and mistrust.
- **Equity demands inclusion.** Families with less exposure to technology -- including many immigrant and non-English-speaking families -- are most likely to be left out of AI conversations.

The sessions that generated this data were designed to address that gap. The surveys capture a snapshot of where parents stood *before* receiving any formal AI education from their schools.

---

## What We Asked

The survey measured six dimensions:

1. **AI Familiarity** -- How familiar are you with artificial intelligence?
2. **Attitude** -- How do you feel about AI being used in your child's education? (Supportive / Curious / Cautious / Concerned)
3. **Comfort** -- How comfortable are you with your child using AI tools?
4. **Concerns** -- What worries you most about AI in education?
5. **Desired Outcomes** -- What do you want your child to learn about AI?
6. **Personal Use** -- Do you personally use AI tools?

Parents also indicated the grade level of their child (TK-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-12).

---

## What We Found

### Finding 1: Most Parents Have Heard of AI, but Few Understand It

| Familiarity Level | Responses | Percentage |
|-------------------|-----------|------------|
| Somewhat familiar -- heard of it but don't use much | 66 | 58.4% |
| Very familiar -- use it often | 35 | 31.0% |
| Not familiar -- don't really know what it is | 12 | 10.6% |

Nearly 6 in 10 parents have heard of AI but aren't actively using it. About a third consider themselves regular users. One in ten has little to no awareness. Parents are not starting from zero -- most have some exposure through tools like Siri, Alexa, or ChatGPT -- but there is a significant gap between awareness ("I've heard of it") and understanding ("I can explain what it does and how it works").

Familiarity also increases with the age of the child. Parents of high schoolers (9-12) were nearly twice as likely to report being "very familiar" (43.8%) compared to parents of the youngest students in TK-2 (24.4%) -- likely because older children bring more AI exposure home through school and social media.

**Implication:** Parent PD sessions should not start with "What is AI?" in the abstract. Start with the AI their kids are already using -- ChatGPT, Snapchat AI, Google's AI features -- and build understanding from there.

### Finding 2: Parents Are Curious but Cautious

| Attitude | Responses | Percentage |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| Cautious -- want to know more before supporting it | 49 | 47.6% |
| Excited -- see potential benefits for students | 24 | 23.3% |
| Concerned -- worried about risks | 19 | 18.4% |
| Unsure -- don't know enough yet | 11 | 10.7% |

The dominant attitude was not fear or opposition -- it was cautious curiosity. Nearly half of parents (47.6%) want more information before forming a firm opinion. Combined with the "excited" group, **70.9% of parents lean positive or open**. Only 18.4% are actively concerned, and 10.7% feel they don't know enough to have an opinion.

Here's what's striking: **familiarity predicts attitude.** Among parents who reported being "very familiar" with AI, 51.5% were excited. Among parents who were "not familiar," 80% were either concerned or unsure. This suggests that exposure and education can shift attitudes from apprehension toward openness.

| Grade Band | Excited | Cautious | Concerned | Unsure |
|------------|---------|----------|-----------|--------|
| TK-2 | 19.5% | 48.8% | 12.2% | 19.5% |
| 3-5 | 23.3% | 46.7% | 20.0% | 10.0% |
| 6-8 | 23.3% | 55.8% | 11.6% | 9.3% |
| 9-12 | 31.2% | 40.6% | 21.9% | 6.2% |

Excitement increases with grade level (19.5% to 31.2%), while uncertainty drops (19.5% to 6.2%). Parents of older students have had more time to form opinions -- for better or worse.

**Implication:** Districts have a time-limited opportunity. Parents are willing to learn right now. The longer schools wait to engage parents on AI, the more likely attitudes will calcify around fear or misinformation.

### Finding 3: Privacy and Overreliance Are the Top Concerns

| Concern | % of Parents |
|---------|-------------|
| Plagiarism or overreliance on AI | **75.7%** |
| Privacy and safety of student data | **60.2%** |
| Accuracy of information (wrong answers) | 52.4% |
| Too much screen time | 42.7% |
| Bias or fairness in AI tools | 36.9% |

Parents selected an average of 2.8 concerns each -- they're not worried about one thing, they're worried about several. But the top two are clear:

1. **Plagiarism and overreliance** (75.7%) -- Will my child stop thinking for themselves?
2. **Data privacy** (60.2%) -- Who has access to my child's data?

These concerns are rational, specific, and addressable. They are not anti-technology sentiment -- they are the concerns of engaged parents who want guardrails.

Concerns also shift by grade level:

| Concern | TK-2 | 3-5 | 6-8 | 9-12 |
|---------|------|-----|-----|------|
| Plagiarism/overreliance | 61.0% | 80.0% | 86.0% | 84.4% |
| Privacy/data safety | 61.0% | 60.0% | 53.5% | 59.4% |
| Accuracy of info | 43.9% | 60.0% | 51.2% | 59.4% |
| Screen time | 46.3% | 36.7% | 39.5% | 34.4% |
| Bias/fairness | 29.3% | 33.3% | 39.5% | 46.9% |

Plagiarism concern spikes from 61% (TK-2) to 86% (6-8) as children face more independent academic work. Screen time concern decreases with age. Bias/fairness awareness nearly doubles from youngest to oldest, suggesting parents of older students are more attuned to algorithmic issues.

**Implication:** Address privacy and academic integrity head-on in every parent communication about AI. Don't bury these topics -- lead with them. Show parents the specific safeguards in place (content filters, monitoring, acceptable use policies). If those safeguards don't exist yet, that's a problem to fix before scaling AI adoption.

### Finding 4: Most Parents Are Not Using AI Themselves

From a subset of 10 parents surveyed in January 2026 who were asked directly about AI tool usage:

| Response | Count | Percentage |
|----------|-------|------------|
| Yes -- sometimes | 4 | 40.0% |
| No -- not yet | 3 | 30.0% |
| Not sure | 2 | 20.0% |
| Yes -- often | 1 | 10.0% |

Half of children have used AI tools at least sometimes, but 20% of parents weren't sure whether their child had used AI at all -- a concerning awareness gap. In the larger sample, the fact that 58% of parents describe themselves as only "somewhat familiar" with AI, combined with only 10.7% feeling "very comfortable" with their child using it, suggests that most parents are being asked to form opinions about something they have limited personal experience with.

**Implication:** The most impactful thing a parent PD session can do is put AI in parents' hands. Let them try ChatGPT. Let them ask it a question in their language. Let them see what their kids are seeing. Hands-on experience shifts the conversation from abstract fear to concrete evaluation.

### Finding 5: Parents Want Responsible Use, Not Prohibition

**What parents want their children to gain from AI:**

| Learning Goal | % of Parents |
|---------------|-------------|
| Extra support in difficult subjects | **69.9%** |
| Preparing for future jobs and tech skills | 50.5% |
| Personalized learning at their own pace | 42.7% |
| Reducing busywork for higher-level thinking | 37.9% |

**What parents want to learn themselves:**

| Desired Outcome | % of Parents |
|-----------------|-------------|
| How AI can help students learn at home and school | **79.6%** |
| What guardrails/rules protect students | **78.6%** |
| How families can guide responsible AI use | 69.9% |
| Basics of how AI works | 56.3% |

Parents prioritize practical, safety-focused information over technical knowledge. They want to know what AI does for their kids and what protections exist -- not how the algorithms work. And when it comes to their children, they consistently prioritized responsible use over avoidance. They want their kids to:

- Know how to use AI as a tool, not a crutch
- Understand when AI is helpful and when it's not
- Develop critical thinking about AI-generated content
- Be prepared for a workforce that includes AI

Very few parents expressed a desire to keep AI out of schools entirely. The overwhelming sentiment was: *teach my child to use it well.*

**Implication:** Frame AI education as a life skill, not a threat response. Parents are already there -- they understand AI isn't going away. They want schools to prepare their kids, not protect them from it.

## Recommendations for Districts

Based on these findings, we offer six recommendations for school districts planning AI integration:

### 1. Don't Wait to Engage Parents

The data shows parents are in a window of curious openness. This window closes as uninformed opinions harden. Launch parent AI sessions now, even if your AI policies aren't finalized. Parents would rather be part of the conversation than informed after the fact.

### 2. Make It Hands-On

Lectures about AI don't change attitudes. Hands-on demonstrations do. Let parents use AI tools during the session. Let them see what their children are seeing. Provide guided prompts they can try at home. The single biggest shift in the sessions that generated this data came when parents opened ChatGPT for the first time.

### 3. Lead with Concerns, Not Features

Don't start with "AI is amazing." Start with "Here's what you're worried about, and here's what we're doing about it." Address privacy policies, academic integrity tools, and content filtering before showcasing instructional benefits. Parents need to trust the safety before they can appreciate the potential.

### 4. Communicate in the Languages Your Community Speaks

In districts with significant non-English-speaking populations, AI communications must go beyond translation. Concepts like "machine learning" and "generative AI" need to be explained in accessible terms in every language your community speaks. The PD sessions that generated this data were conducted bilingually, which was critical to participation and comprehension.

### 5. Position Parents as Partners, Not Audiences

Parents have valuable perspectives on AI -- about risk, about their children, about what matters. Surveys like the one used here give parents a voice. But voice without influence is performative. Include parent representatives in AI policy development. Share survey results back with the community. Show parents that their input shaped decisions.

### 6. Differentiate by Grade Level

The data suggests that parent concerns and priorities may shift depending on the age of their child. Parents of younger children may focus more on screen time and age-appropriateness, while parents of older students may prioritize academic integrity and career readiness. Tailor messaging accordingly.

## Limitations

This brief reports on program evaluation data, not a controlled research study. Several limitations should be noted:

- **Convenience sample:** Participants were parents who attended voluntary PD sessions and may not represent the broader parent population.
- **Pre-session data only:** These findings reflect where parents stood before receiving AI education. The near-absence of post-session data (only 1 post-survey response out of 113 pre-survey respondents) means we cannot measure the impact of the sessions themselves.
- **No individual demographics:** The survey did not collect race/ethnicity, income, education level, or language preference at the individual level. Community-level demographics provide context but cannot be attributed to individual respondents.
- **No matched identifiers:** Without unique participant IDs, pre and post responses cannot be linked for individual-level change analysis.
- **Single region:** All data comes from two school districts in Southern California. Generalization to other regions should be made cautiously.

These limitations are common in program evaluation contexts and do not diminish the practical value of the findings. They do, however, point to opportunities for more rigorous research (see below).

---

## What's Next: Building the Evidence Base

This program evaluation surfaced important patterns, but it also revealed gaps in the data infrastructure that, if addressed, could support formal research and publication. We recommend:

1. **Add demographics to the survey.** Race/ethnicity, home language, education level, and household income would enable more nuanced analysis and support equity-focused research.
2. **Add unique identifiers.** An anonymous ID (even a self-generated code) would allow pre/post matching without collecting personally identifiable information.
3. **Prioritize post-session data collection.** The 113-to-1 pre-to-post ratio suggests a process problem, not a parent problem. Build the post-survey into the session itself -- administer it before parents leave.
4. **Expand to more districts.** A multi-district dataset with consistent instrumentation would be publishable and valuable to the field.
5. **Seek IRB review for future administrations.** If the goal is to publish in peer-reviewed journals, prospective IRB approval (or exempt determination) should be obtained before the next data collection cycle.
