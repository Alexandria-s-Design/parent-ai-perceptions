# What 113 Parents Told Us About AI in Their Children's Schools

*By William Gideon Jr., Ed.D.*

Between May 2025 and January 2026, I facilitated a series of parent-facing professional development sessions on artificial intelligence at schools in Culver City USD and Lynwood USD in Southern California. Before each session, parents completed a survey measuring their familiarity with AI, their attitudes, their concerns, and what they wanted to learn.

One hundred thirteen parents responded. What they told us should shape how every district approaches AI communication with families.

## Parents Are Not Against AI. They Are Uninformed About It.

Fifty-eight percent of parents described themselves as "somewhat familiar" with AI -- they had heard of it but were not using it. Thirty-one percent said they were very familiar. About one in ten said they did not really know what AI was.

The gap between hearing about AI and understanding it is wide. Most parents have encountered Siri, Alexa, or a ChatGPT headline, but few can explain what these tools do or how they work in a classroom. Parents of older students reported higher familiarity -- 43.8 percent of parents with high schoolers said they were very familiar, compared with 24.4 percent of parents with children in TK through second grade. Older children bring more AI exposure home.

## Seventy-One Percent Lean Open

When asked how they felt about AI in their children's education, the most common response was not fear. It was cautious curiosity.

Nearly half of parents -- 47.6 percent -- said they wanted to know more before forming an opinion. Another 23.3 percent said they were excited about the potential. Combined, 70.9 percent of parents were either open or positive. Only 18.4 percent described themselves as concerned, and 10.7 percent said they were unsure.

Here is what stood out most: familiarity predicted attitude. Among parents who reported being very familiar with AI, 51.5 percent were excited. Among parents who were not familiar at all, 80 percent were either concerned or unsure. The data suggests that education moves parents from apprehension toward openness.

Districts have a limited window. Parents are willing to learn right now. The longer schools wait, the more likely attitudes harden around fear or misinformation.

## What Parents Worry About

Parents were asked to select their concerns from a list, and they selected an average of 2.8 each. The top five:

- Plagiarism or overreliance on AI: 75.7 percent
- Privacy and safety of student data: 60.2 percent
- Accuracy of information: 52.4 percent
- Too much screen time: 42.7 percent
- Bias or fairness in AI tools: 36.9 percent

These are not irrational fears. They are the concerns of engaged parents who want guardrails. Plagiarism worry spiked from 61 percent among parents of the youngest children to 86 percent among parents of middle schoolers, where independent academic work increases. Bias and fairness awareness nearly doubled from TK-2 (29.3 percent) to high school (46.9 percent).

## What Parents Actually Want

When asked what they hoped to learn from the session, parents did not ask for technical explanations of how AI works. They asked for practical information:

- How AI can help students learn at home and school: 79.6 percent
- What guardrails and rules protect students: 78.6 percent
- How families can guide responsible AI use: 69.9 percent
- Basics of how AI works: 56.3 percent

And when asked what they wanted for their children, the top response was extra support in difficult subjects (69.9 percent), followed by preparation for future jobs (50.5 percent) and personalized learning (42.7 percent).

Very few parents wanted AI removed from schools. The overwhelming message was: teach my child to use it well.

## What Districts Should Do

Six recommendations from this data:

**Start now.** The window of curious openness will close. Launch parent AI sessions even if your policies are not finalized. Parents would rather be part of the conversation than informed after the fact.

**Make it hands-on.** The biggest shift in our sessions came when parents opened ChatGPT for the first time. Let them try it. Let them ask a question in their own language. Hands-on experience changes the conversation from abstract fear to concrete evaluation.

**Lead with concerns.** Do not start with what AI can do. Start with what parents are worried about and what you are doing about it. Address privacy, academic integrity, and content filtering before showcasing benefits.

**Communicate in the languages your community speaks.** In our sessions, bilingual delivery was critical to participation and comprehension. Translating a flyer is not enough. Concepts like "machine learning" need to be explained in accessible terms in every language your families speak.

**Include parents in decisions.** Surveys give parents a voice, but voice without influence is performative. Share results back with the community. Include parent representatives in AI policy development.

**Differentiate by grade level.** Parents of younger children worry about screen time and age-appropriateness. Parents of older students worry about cheating and career readiness. One message does not fit all.

## The Bigger Picture

This data comes from program evaluation, not a controlled study. The sample is limited to parents who chose to attend these sessions, and we did not collect individual demographic information. But the patterns are clear and consistent across 113 responses: parents are not opposed to AI. They are waiting for someone to include them in the conversation.

The question for districts is not whether to engage parents on AI. It is whether they will do it proactively -- or wait until the backlash arrives.
