What 113 Parents Told Us About AI in Their Children's Schools

A Program Evaluation Brief from Parent AI Professional Development Sessions in Southern California

William Gideon Jr., Ed.D.

The Right Path Educational Consulting (TRPEC) | Southern California

Overview

Between May 2025 and January 2026, The Right Path Educational Consulting (TRPEC) facilitated a series of parent-facing professional development sessions on artificial intelligence in K-12 education at schools in Culver City Unified School District and Lynwood Unified School District, California. Before each session, parents completed a structured survey measuring their familiarity with AI, attitudes toward AI in their children's education, comfort levels, concerns, and desired learning outcomes.

This brief presents findings from 113 parent responses collected across two survey administrations. It is intended to help school districts, parent engagement coordinators, and educational technology leaders understand what parents are thinking and feeling about AI -- and how to design better engagement around it.

Note on community context: The participating districts serve diverse communities. Lynwood USD enrolls a student population that is approximately 97% Hispanic/Latino, and Culver City USD serves a mixed-demographic community. Many participating families include English Language Learners. These demographic characteristics are relevant context for interpreting the findings, though individual demographic data was not collected in the survey instrument.

Why This Matters

AI tools are entering classrooms faster than schools can develop policies around them. Teachers are being trained. Students are experimenting. But in most districts, parents are the last to know. They hear about AI from the news, from their kids, or not at all.

  • Parents control the home learning environment. If they don't understand AI, they can't guide their children's use of it.
  • Parent buy-in determines policy success. AI integration efforts that ignore parents risk backlash, opt-outs, and mistrust.
  • Equity demands inclusion. Families with less exposure to technology -- including many immigrant and non-English-speaking families -- are most likely to be left out of AI conversations.

What We Found

Finding 1: Most Parents Have Heard of AI, but Few Understand It

58%Somewhat familiar
31%Very familiar
11%Not familiar

Nearly 6 in 10 parents have heard of AI but aren't actively using it. There is a significant gap between awareness ("I've heard of it") and understanding ("I can explain what it does and how it works"). Familiarity increases with the age of the child -- parents of high schoolers were nearly twice as likely to report being "very familiar" (43.8%) compared to parents of TK-2 students (24.4%).

Implication: Don't start with "What is AI?" in the abstract. Start with the AI their kids are already using -- ChatGPT, Snapchat AI, Google's AI features.

The dominant attitude was not fear or opposition -- it was cautious curiosity. Combined with the "excited" group, 70.9% of parents lean positive or open.

Here's what's striking: familiarity predicts attitude. Among parents who reported being "very familiar" with AI, 51.5% were excited. Parents selected an average of 2.8 concerns each. These concerns are rational, specific, and addressable. They are not anti-technology sentiment -- they are the concerns of engaged parents who want guardrails.

Plagiarism concern spikes from 61% (TK-2) to 86% (6-8) as children face more independent academic work. Bias/fairness awareness nearly doubles from youngest (29.3%) to oldest (46.9%).

Finding 4: Most Parents Are Not Using AI Themselves

In a January 2026 subset, 20% of parents weren't sure whether their child had even used AI -- a concerning awareness gap. In the larger sample, 58% describe themselves as only "somewhat familiar," and only 10.7% feel "very comfortable" with their child using AI.

Implication: Put AI in parents' hands. Let them try ChatGPT during the session. Let them ask it a question in their language. Hands-on experience shifts the conversation from abstract fear to concrete evaluation.

Finding 5: Parents Want Responsible Use, Not Prohibition

What Parents Want to Learn% of Parents

Parents prioritize practical, safety-focused information over technical knowledge. Very few expressed a desire to keep AI out of schools entirely. The overwhelming sentiment was: teach my child to use it well.

Implication: Frame AI education as a life skill, not a threat response. Parents understand AI isn't going away. They want schools to prepare their kids, not protect them from it.

Recommendations for Districts

1 Don't Wait to Engage Parents

The data shows parents are in a window of curious openness. This window closes as uninformed opinions harden. Launch parent AI sessions now, even if your AI policies aren't finalized.

2 Make It Hands-On

Lectures don't change attitudes. Hands-on demonstrations do. Let parents use AI tools during the session. The single biggest shift came when parents opened ChatGPT for the first time.

3 Lead with Concerns, Not Features

Don't start with "AI is amazing." Start with "Here's what you're worried about, and here's what we're doing about it." Parents need to trust the safety before they can appreciate the potential.

4 Communicate in the Languages Your Community Speaks

AI communications must go beyond translation. Concepts like "machine learning" and "generative AI" need to be explained in accessible terms in every language your community speaks.

5 Position Parents as Partners, Not Audiences

Include parent representatives in AI policy development. Share survey results back with the community. Show parents that their input shaped decisions.

6 Differentiate by Grade Level

Parents of younger children focus more on screen time and age-appropriateness. Parents of older students prioritize academic integrity and career readiness. Tailor messaging accordingly.

Limitations

  • Convenience sample: Participants attended voluntary PD sessions and may not represent the broader parent population.
  • Pre-session data only: Only 1 post-survey response was collected out of 113 pre-survey respondents.
  • No individual demographics: Race/ethnicity, income, and language preference were not collected at the individual level.
  • Single region: All data comes from two Southern California districts.

What's Next

  1. Add demographics to the survey for more nuanced analysis
  2. Add unique identifiers for pre/post matching
  3. Prioritize post-session data collection -- administer before parents leave
  4. Expand to more districts for broader generalizability
  5. Seek IRB review for future administrations to support peer-reviewed publication
