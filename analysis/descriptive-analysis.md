## 11. Key Findings Summary

### Research Question 1: What is the distribution of AI familiarity among parents?
The majority (58-60%) are "somewhat familiar" -- they have heard of AI but do not actively use it. About one-third are active users. Roughly 10% have little to no awareness. Familiarity correlates positively with children's grade level.

### Research Question 2: What attitudes do parents hold toward AI in education?
The dominant attitude is **cautious openness** (47.6%). Combined with excited parents (23.3%), nearly 71% of parents are receptive to AI if properly informed. Attitudes are strongly linked to familiarity -- more familiar parents are dramatically more excited, while unfamiliar parents are predominantly concerned or unsure.

### Research Question 3: What are the most common concerns?
1. Plagiarism/overreliance on AI (75-80%)
2. Privacy and data security (60%)
3. Accuracy of information (52-60%)
4. Screen time (42.7%)
5. Bias/fairness (36.9%)

### Research Question 4: Do attitudes differ by grade level of child?
Yes. Parents of older students (9-12) are more excited about AI (31.2% vs. 19.5% for TK-2) and less unsure (6.2% vs. 19.5%). However, plagiarism concerns spike dramatically for grades 3+ (80-86% vs. 61% for TK-2), and bias/fairness concerns grow with grade level.

### Research Question 5: What do parents want to learn about AI?
Parents prioritize **practical, safety-focused information**: how AI helps learning (79.6%), what protections exist (78.6%), and how to guide responsible use at home (69.9%). Technical understanding of how AI works is the lowest priority (56.3%).

### Research Question 6: How comfortable are parents with their children using AI?
Approximately 57% are at least somewhat comfortable, while 43% are not. Comfort levels are relatively stable across grade levels, though TK-2 parents show the highest discomfort (22% "not comfortable at all"). The January 2026 cohort was notably less comfortable (70% not comfortable).

---

## 12. Implications for Program Design

1. **Lead with safety and guardrails**: Parents' top priority is understanding protections, not technical AI knowledge. Workshops should frontload responsible use frameworks.

2. **Address plagiarism/overreliance directly**: As the #1 concern (75-80%), this needs dedicated workshop time with concrete strategies parents can use at home.

3. **Differentiate by grade band**: Younger-child parents need more foundational AI literacy and screen time guidance. Older-child parents need academic integrity strategies and career readiness framing.

4. **Familiarity breeds acceptance**: The strong familiarity-to-excitement correlation suggests that hands-on AI experiences during workshops could shift cautious parents toward openness.

5. **Close the awareness gap**: 20% of parents don't know if their child has used AI. Awareness-building about common student AI tools should be part of every session.
